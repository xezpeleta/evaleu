# **Comprehensive Evaluation of Large Language Models and Benchmarking Resources for the Basque Language**

The rapid and disruptive evolution of generative artificial intelligence has fundamentally altered the trajectory of natural language processing research and industrial application. The proliferation of Large Language Models has established entirely new performance standards across a multitude of cognitive, semantic, and linguistic tasks, shifting the paradigm from task-specific architectures to generalized, few-shot learners. However, the foundational architectures driving this paradigm shift remain overwhelmingly English-centric, heavily relying on massive internet-scale corpora dominated by high-resource languages.1 This structural and computational bias exacerbates the technological and digital divide, leaving low-resource, marginalized, and linguistically isolated languages at a severe disadvantage. The Basque language, a pre-Indo-European isolate characterized by a complex agglutinative morphology and ergative-absolutive alignment, represents approximately 0.035% of the total content in global repositories such as Common Crawl.1 By comparison, English constitutes nearly half of this repository. When subjected to zero-shot or few-shot prompts in low-resource environments, traditional open-weight multilingual models frequently exhibit performance metrics that barely surpass random chance, exposing a critical fragility in their generalization capabilities.3  
To bridge this persistent digital divide, specialized research initiatives, primarily driven by the HiTZ Center for Language Technology at the University of the Basque Country (UPV/EHU) and Orai NLP Technologies, have emerged. These initiatives focus on the continual pretraining, instruction tuning, rigorous alignment, and exhaustive evaluation of Large Language Models tailored specifically for the Basque language.4 The development of robust, localized evaluation frameworks is equally critical, as assessing linguistic competence, cultural alignment, and reasoning capabilities in a low-resource setting requires natively sourced benchmarks that avoid the artifacts introduced by machine-translated datasets.1 This comprehensive report delivers an exhaustive analysis of the contemporary ecosystem of Basque Large Language Models, detailing foundational model architectures, advanced multimodal integrations, state-of-the-art evaluation datasets, and the most recent academic discourse shaping the field of low-resource natural language processing.

## **Foundational Architectures and Continual Pretraining for Basque**

The strategic imperative to develop sovereign language technologies has led to the creation of bespoke open-weight models designed to process and generate Basque text with high fluency, structural accuracy, and factual reliability. The prevailing methodological approach leverages existing high-resource foundational models, adapting them through continual pretraining on highly curated, localized Basque corpora.4 Training a competitive model entirely from scratch for a language with limited digital representation is widely considered computationally inefficient and data-starved. Therefore, the transfer learning paradigm is leveraged to exploit the deep reasoning and logical structures already embedded within high-resource models.

### **The Latxa Model Family: Scaling Basque NLP**

Developed by the HiTZ Center for Language Technology, the Latxa family represents a cornerstone achievement in Basque natural language processing.4 Latxa is an open-source suite of large language models ranging from 7 billion to 70 billion parameters, specifically engineered to overcome the limitations of default multilingual models.8 Rather than initializing parameters randomly, the architecture is fundamentally based on Meta’s LLaMA 2 and subsequent Llama 3.1 models, incorporating extensive continual pretraining to map the model's latent space to Basque linguistic structures.4  
The pretraining phase utilized the Latxa Corpus v1.1, an extensively deduplicated and meticulously filtered dataset comprising 4.3 million documents and 4.2 billion tokens.4 A significant and foundational portion of this data was derived from EusCrawl, a high-quality corpus constructed via ad-hoc scrapers targeting 33 prominent Basque websites.9 This targeted scraping approach yielded 1.72 million documents and 288 million words, resulting in cleaner, more formalized text compared to general-purpose web crawls that often suffer from noise, poor optical character recognition, and cross-lingual contamination.9  
To mitigate catastrophic forgetting—a well-documented phenomenon where continual pretraining on a new, narrow domain or language erodes the generalized reasoning, instruction-following, and safety capabilities embedded during the original high-resource training—the training mixture for Latxa maintained a strict 80:20 ratio of Basque to English data.2 This carefully calibrated data mixture ensures that the models acquire robust Basque linguistic competence while simultaneously preserving the latent logical capabilities and cross-lingual transfer efficiencies inherited from the vast English pretraining distributions.1 Empirical evidence demonstrated that retaining this English data was critical for strong few-shot performance; early ablations of the 7B model trained exclusively on Basque suffered severe degradation in cross-lingual benchmarks like BeleBele, losing up to 13 performance points.1  
The environmental and computational economics of training the Latxa family have been transparently documented, contributing to the broader discourse on Green AI. The training was conducted on the CINECA Leonardo supercomputer under the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking.1 The computational burden and subsequent carbon footprint scale exponentially with parameter size.

### **The Llama-eus Architecture and Sub-10B Optimization**

Operating parallel to the Latxa initiative, Orai NLP Technologies engineered the Llama-eus-8B model, aiming to establish a highly efficient, state-of-the-art baseline specifically within the sub-10 billion parameter category.2 Adapted from Meta’s advanced Llama-3.1-8B architecture, this model directly addresses the functional and formal linguistic competence deficits frequently observed when applying out-of-the-box multilingual architectures to Basque text generation and comprehension.7  
Llama-eus-8B underwent continual pretraining on the ZelaiHandi dataset, which acts as the largest collection of freely licensed and clean Basque texts available, aggregating approximately 1.5 billion tokens across 521 million words.2 To prevent the aforementioned catastrophic forgetting, this corpus was strategically combined with a 300 million token subset of the English FineWeb dataset.7 The ZelaiHandi corpus explicitly prioritizes formal, well-formed, and openly licensed text to ensure optimal linguistic adaptation.2 Comprehensive pipeline analyses revealed that this targeted continual pretraining phase yielded a direct 12-point improvement in natural language understanding metrics, definitively proving that exposure to high-quality, linguistically rigorous text significantly restructures the model's internal representations to favor the target language’s complex syntax.2

## **Instruction Tuning and Human Preference Alignment in Low-Resource Contexts**

Developing a base model capable of accurately predicting the next token in a Basque sequence represents only the first half of the contemporary Large Language Model pipeline. For a model to be practically deployable as a chatbot, semantic search engine, or automated assistant, it must subsequently be aligned to function according to user intent. The systematic study of instructing LLMs for low-resource languages poses a profound challenge, primarily because large-scale, human-annotated instruction datasets are abundant in English but virtually nonexistent for Basque.12  
The Latxa-Instruct project was initiated to address this exact bottleneck, providing an open-source framework for reproducible training and benchmarking of Basque instruction-tuned models.12 Researchers confronted the core hypothesis of whether synthetic data generation and automated translation could adequately replace organic human annotation in a low-resource setting. The experimental framework generated large-scale instruction-response pairs via the Magpie synthetic data generation tool, alongside direct, high-fidelity neural translations of established English instruction datasets.12  
The findings derived from evaluating these synthetic pipelines comprehensively demonstrate that aligning a low-resource model utilizing a mixture of translated English instructions and synthetically generated Basque instructions yields highly performant conversational agents.2 For instance, applying this automated instruction tuning to the Llama-eus-8B base model resulted in an unprecedented 24-point improvement in instruction-following performance.2  
However, synthetic instructions alone cannot capture the nuance of cultural values, safety constraints, and conversational naturalness required for true human alignment. Consequently, the HiTZ Zentroa team developed the first native human preference dataset for Basque, termed *Ebaluatoia*.12 This monumental resource comprises 12,890 preference annotations compiled from 1,285 human participants, detailing real user prompts, model responses, and ranked preferences across 21 different LLMs, including proprietary frontier models like GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet.12 This dataset allows researchers to apply advanced alignment techniques, such as Direct Preference Optimization, ensuring the model's outputs are not only grammatically correct but culturally and contextually appropriate for the Basque-speaking demographic.  
An essential, paradigm-shifting insight derived from this instruction-tuning research concerns the choice of the foundational backbone model. Rigorous ablation studies indicated that utilizing an already instruction-tuned high-resource model (such as Llama 3.1 Instruct) as the backbone for continual pretraining consistently outperforms using a raw base model, even when the target language is entirely new to the instruction distribution.12 The underlying logic dictates that when a foundational model has already internalized the complex mathematical vectors of human alignment, formatting logic, and dialogue turn-taking in English, adapting those specific behaviors to Basque requires significantly less computational effort and instructional data.13 The algorithmic understanding of "how to act as an assistant" transfers across linguistic barriers more efficiently than the acquisition of raw vocabulary. By scaling this approach to a 70-billion parameter backbone, researchers produced an instructed Latxa model that approaches the capabilities of massively funded frontier models, entirely circumventing the need for native human-authored instruction data during the initial supervised fine-tuning phase.13

## **The Emergence of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)**

As the natural language processing domain transitions toward comprehensive multimodal understanding, the frontiers of Basque NLP have necessarily expanded beyond text. Multimodal Large Language Models, which seamlessly integrate textual reasoning with image, video, and audio processing capabilities, currently dominate the commercial artificial intelligence landscape.14 However, while commercial MLLMs deliver acceptable zero-shot performance in low-resource languages through massive multilingual scaling, comparable open-source results have remained historically unattained.14  
Recent research executed by the HiTZ Center tackled this disparity by establishing a late-fusion MLLM pipeline specifically tailored for Basque.14 The primary obstacle to this development was the absolute absence of native image-text pairs required to align visual encoders with Basque text decoders. To bypass this limitation without relying on proprietary commercial systems, researchers generated over 3 million synthetic multimodal training instances through sophisticated neural translation of English-centric datasets.14 This translation pipeline utilized sentence-level neural machine translation tailored specifically for multimodal contexts, generating extensive training corpora alongside 8,000 human-validated evaluation instances.14  
The subsequent training and evaluation phase relied on a late-fusion paradigm, adapting pretrained LLMs to process visual tokens.14 Extensive ablation studies on the data mixtures yielded highly counterintuitive findings that carry immense strategic implications for all low-resource multimodal NLP research. First, the integration of merely 20% of Basque multimodal data—with the remaining 80% remaining in English—proved sufficient to achieve robust, highly performant results on localized Basque visual benchmarks.14 This indicates a rapid saturation point for visual-linguistic alignment in secondary languages.  
Second, the experiments indicated that a natively instructed Basque backbone LLM is not strictly required to build a strong MLLM for Basque. Contrary to prevailing expectations, an English-centric backbone model (such as Llama 3.1 Instruct), when exposed to the 20% Basque multimodal data mixture, matched the performance of specialized Basque backbones (such as Latxa-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) on open-ended visual question-answering tasks.14 These two configurations exhibited comparable performance in 54% of the open-ended benchmarks.14 This suggests that visual-semantic alignment mechanisms operate primarily at the latent conceptual level; the model maps the visual features of an object directly to a shared conceptual space, bridging language representations efficiently and minimizing the necessity for exhaustive, native-language multimodal pretraining.14 The implications of this finding are profound, as sourcing massive volumes of aligned image-text data for a language like Basque is exponentially more difficult than acquiring text-only corpora.

## **Comprehensive Evaluation Frameworks: The BasqueGLUE Benchmark**

Robust, exhaustive empirical evaluation forms the bedrock of credible machine learning research. Historically, the evaluation of low-resource languages relied on translated variants of established English benchmarks, such as MMLU or the General Language Understanding Evaluation (GLUE) benchmark. However, automated translation artifacts frequently skew these evaluation metrics, penalizing models for processing unnatural syntactic structures introduced by the translator rather than measuring genuine reasoning deficits. Consequently, comprehensive, natively sourced evaluation suites have been systematically developed for the Basque language to ensure accurate architectural validation.  
The BasqueGLUE benchmark is the premier, multitask evaluation suite designed to assess natural language understanding in Basque.15 Modeled structurally and philosophically after the English GLUE and SuperGLUE frameworks, it evaluates the capacity of neural models to capture deep linguistic semantics, contextual dependencies, and logical entailment, moving far beyond superficial token matching heuristics.15 The benchmark aggregates nine distinct sequence-level and token-level classification tasks across highly varied domains, including Wikipedia articles, formal journalistic news, and highly informal social media discourse.

The construction of BasqueGLUE demonstrates careful, manual curation designed to prevent neural architectures from relying on statistical heuristics or dataset biases.18 The Named Entity Recognition tasks (NERCid and NERCood) merge data from the standard EIEC dataset with newer annotations from the Basque newspaper *Naiz* and Wikipedia, utilizing the BIO annotation scheme to categorize persons, organizations, locations, and miscellaneous entities.18 This enforces robustness across both formal reporting and encyclopedic prose.  
In the realm of conversational artificial intelligence, the FMTODeu\_intent and FMTODeu\_slot datasets test a model's utility in task-oriented dialogue systems.17 Translated and adapted from the Facebook Multilingual Task Oriented Dataset, these resources require the LLM to classify user utterances into categories like alarms, reminders, or weather queries, and subsequently extract specific entity slots, an essential capability for localized digital assistants.17  
The inclusion of highly colloquial and domain-specific social media datasets poses a severe test for modern language models. The BEC2016eu task evaluates sentiment analysis capabilities on tweets collected during the 2016 Basque election campaign, forcing models to interpret political slang, colloquialisms, and implicit biases.17 Similarly, the VaxxStance dataset requires models to perform stance detection regarding the highly controversial anti-vaccine movement.17 Stance detection is a remarkably complex NLP task within the universe of fake news detection; it requires the LLM to discern whether a text implicitly supports, opposes, or remains neutral toward a given topic, often relying on deep sociopolitical context, irony, or sarcasm that traditional sentiment classifiers fail to register.18  
Finally, the QNLIeu task transforms reading comprehension into a binary entailment task.17 Derived from the *ElkarHizketak* conversational QA dataset—which was built by native speaker volunteers extracting dialogues from Basque Wikipedia—the task requires the model to determine whether a given sentence contains the definitive answer to an associated question.17 This mirrors the design of the Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) paradigm and rigorously tests semantic entailment without generative hallucination risks. By spanning this massive variety of formats, BasqueGLUE provides an unimpeachable baseline for evaluating raw language comprehension.

## **The Latxa Evaluation Suite and Advanced Knowledge Benchmarks**

While BasqueGLUE excels at measuring fundamental natural language understanding via classification matrices, the contemporary era of generative Large Language Models demands benchmarks that evaluate broader world knowledge, multi-step reasoning, and complex reading comprehension within zero-shot and few-shot paradigms. To address this necessity, the researchers behind the Latxa project introduced a massive new suite of multiple-choice evaluation datasets comprising 23,282 questions.3 This suite operates analogously to the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark in English, facilitating structured, automated evaluation via the EleutherAI LM Evaluation Harness.1

| Dataset Identifier | Total Questions | Core Evaluation Focus | Source Material Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **EusProficiency** | 5,169 | Formal linguistic competence | Questions extracted from official Basque language proficiency examinations. |
| **EusReading** | 352 | Reading comprehension | Diverse textual passages requiring contextual synthesis and inference. |
| **EusTrivia** | 1,715 | General and localized knowledge | Trivia queries spanning five distinct academic and cultural areas. |
| **EusExams** | 16,046 | Advanced professional expertise | Public service examinations from local Basque civic institutions. |

The strategic value of the Latxa Evaluation Suite extends far beyond its volume; its primary utility lies in its cultural and localized relevance.1 The EusExams dataset is particularly notable. It comprises over 16,000 questions designed to prepare candidates for highly specialized public service examinations conducted by specific Basque institutions, including the public health system (Osakidetza), the Basque Government, the City Councils of Bilbao and Vitoria-Gasteiz, and the University of the Basque Country.6 These questions evaluate a model's retention of knowledge specific to local civic administration, healthcare regulations, and regional academic standards. Such nuanced, localized data fundamentally cannot be accurately assessed through translated English benchmarks, rendering EusExams an indispensable tool for measuring an LLM's true utility within the Basque socioeconomic infrastructure.6  
In exhaustive empirical evaluations, the Latxa models consistently and substantially outperformed all existing open-source counterparts on these specific datasets, validating the efficacy of the massive continual pretraining pipeline on the Latxa Corpus.3 More notably, the largest Latxa variants performed competitively with proprietary frontier models, such as OpenAI's GPT-4 Turbo, in the EusProficiency linguistic evaluations.10  
However, despite achieving near parity in formal language proficiency, the open-weight models continued to lag behind frontier models in highly complex reading comprehension and knowledge-intensive retrieval tasks.10 This dichotomy reveals a profound underlying reality regarding the architecture of Large Language Models: structural linguistic competence in a low-resource language (e.g., knowing how to conjugate a Basque verb perfectly) does not automatically guarantee that the model can unlock its latent reasoning capabilities and world knowledge embedded during its initial high-resource English pretraining.10 The reasoning algorithms and vast fact repositories exist within the model's parameters, but the cross-lingual mapping required to articulate that reasoning through the Basque linguistic medium remains an informational bottleneck. Resolving this bottleneck remains the primary objective of ongoing scaling laws research.

## **Specialized Semantic, Sociocultural, and Reasoning Benchmarks**

A mature and responsible natural language processing ecosystem necessitates the rigorous measurement of highly specialized linguistic phenomena, including hallucination rates, sociocultural bias, and physical commonsense reasoning. Relying solely on generalized accuracy metrics masks critical flaws that could render an LLM unsafe or unreliable for public deployment. Consequently, the HiTZ Zentroa team has iteratively published a portfolio of highly specific resources addressing these multifaceted challenges.

### **Evaluating Truthfulness and Hallucination Dynamics**

Large Language Models are notoriously prone to hallucinations—generating fluent but factually incorrect information. The **TruthfulQA-multi** dataset was curated to quantify this phenomenon across linguistic boundaries.19 Extending the foundational English TruthfulQA benchmark via professional translation into Basque, Catalan, Galician, and Spanish, this dataset tests an LLM's susceptibility to "imitative falsehoods".19 Imitative falsehoods are statements that are objectively false but sound highly plausible or mimic common human misconceptions and superstitions.20  
Evaluating models across multiple languages using the identical semantic propositions contained in TruthfulQA-multi uncovers language-specific biases inherent in the model's parametric memory.19 It allows researchers to determine whether an LLM is more likely to hallucinate when prompted in Basque compared to English, thereby testing how well the architecture handles misinformation in non-English, low-resource contexts.19 To facilitate automated evaluation, HiTZ developed specialized judge models, such as the gemma-2-9b-it-multi-truth-judge, which are fine-tuned specifically to score generative responses against the TruthfulQA-multi dataset, minimizing the need for constant human oversight.20

### **Physical Commonsense and Spatial Reasoning**

While LLMs excel at syntactic pattern matching, they often struggle with spatial reasoning and physical commonsense—the intuitive human capacity to understand how real-world objects interact, gravity, temporal sequences, and material properties. The **BasPhyCo** dataset addresses this gap by introducing the first publicly available, non-QA physical commonsense reasoning benchmark in Basque.21  
Crucially, BasPhyCo includes the first such dataset presented in a specific Basque dialect (Western Basque), adding a layer of sociolinguistic complexity to the evaluation.21 Empirical evaluations conducted on this benchmark have definitively demonstrated that modern LLMs generally exhibit severely limited physical commonsense capabilities in low-resource environments.21 When the linguistic data is sparse, the model struggles to construct accurate internal world models, relying instead on superficial token correlations that break down when tested on physical realities.

### **Measuring Sociocultural Bias and Cultural Alignment**

Language models inherently reflect the biases present within their training corpora. To quantify and mitigate these socio-cultural biases, researchers introduced the **BasqBBQ** benchmark, establishing a specialized question-answering framework tailored for assessing social biases specific to the Basque demographic.22  
Furthermore, semantic similarity and cultural knowledge are mapped through culturally grounded datasets such as **BertaQA**.23 BertaQA specifically probes how much internal knowledge an LLM possesses regarding localized Basque culture, history, geography, and literature, contrasting this with generalized Western or Anglo-centric concepts.23 Models that perform exceptionally well on translated trivia often fail spectacularly on BertaQA, revealing that their "world knowledge" is highly localized to the English-speaking world.  
Gender bias in automated translation and generation represents another critical evaluation vector. Utilizing the **FLORES+Gender** dataset, researchers reversed translation directions to analyze whether English-to-Basque translation quality degrades depending on the gender of the referent in the source sentence.24 Utilizing the SacreBLEU toolkit to measure character-level F-scores (chrF++) and Translation Edit Rates (TER), the research assessed how gender bias manifests across different paradigms, including evaluations of the Latxa 3.1 8B Instruct and 70B Instruct models.24 Disaggregating performance by linguistic and contextual factors allows developers to implement targeted debiasing algorithms, ensuring equitable performance across diverse user bases.

## **Dialectal Variability, Code-Switching, and Generative Summarization**

A recurrent and critical blind spot in modern LLM architecture is the fragility of models when confronted with non-standard linguistic variability. The Basque language exhibits immense dialectal variation, which is rarely captured in formalized training corpora heavily skewed toward standard Basque (Batua) found in Wikipedia, administrative texts, or formal news.25  
Academic research conducted in 2025 specifically examined language variability via automated LLM techniques, highlighting severe deficiencies.25 It was observed that proprietary frontier models frequently failed entirely when asked basic questions in specific Basque dialects.25 In documented instances, models like Claude failed to identify the dialectal input out of context, misclassifying the text and bizarrely generating fallback responses in languages such as German, explicitly stating an inability to understand the prompt.25 Only after providing explicit meta-context regarding the language did the models begin to parse the dialect accurately.  
To counter this fragility, researchers have begun cataloging standard-to-dialectal adaptations and integrating diverse datasets into the training pipeline.26 The **BasqueParl** dataset provides transcriptions of parliamentary proceedings, capturing formal spoken language.27 Conversely, the **HelduGazte** and **Covid-19** Twitter datasets introduce highly informal, dynamic speech patterns.27 Most importantly, these social media datasets include high instances of Basque-Spanish code-switching—the fluid alternation between languages within a single utterance or sentence.27 By training and evaluating models on code-switched corpora, LLMs enhance their robustness and more accurately mimic the sociolinguistic realities of bilingual populations. This capability proves essential for downstream NLP tasks, such as precise token language identification and granular stance detection in social media monitoring.27  
In the realm of long-form generative tasks, automated text summarization remains a benchmark of an LLM's ability to compress semantic meaning while retaining core factual propositions. The **BASSE** (BAsque and Spanish Summarization Evaluation) dataset addresses the lack of non-English summarization metrics.28 BASSE collected rigorous human judgments on 2,040 abstractive summaries generated by five different LLMs.28 Annotators evaluated the summaries across five criteria on a Likert scale: coherence, consistency, fluency, relevance, and the extraction of the "5W1H" (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How).28 This human-annotated data is vital for reevaluating traditional automated metrics (like ROUGE) and establishing reliable LLM-as-a-Judge paradigms for Basque text generation. Complementing this is **BasqueSumm**, the first large-scale collection of Basque news documents mapped to their corresponding subheads, serving as an automated proxy for training abstractive summarization models.29

## **Future Trajectories and Systematic Gaps in Low-Resource NLP**

While the empirical progress and architectural innovation in Basque NLP over the analyzed 2024–2026 period is unprecedented, significant systematic gaps remain that dictate the immediate future trajectories of the scientific community.  
First and foremost is the pervasive issue of data contamination and benchmark leakage. As foundational models scrape increasingly vast portions of the internet, the reliance on publicly available translated datasets introduces severe risks.30 If evaluation benchmarks like BasqueGLUE inadvertently leak into the pretraining data, the model will merely memorize the answers rather than demonstrating genuine linguistic reasoning, rendering the evaluation scores artificially inflated and scientifically useless.30 The literature emphasizes the absolute necessity of developing localized, human-curated evaluation suites with strictly held-out, offline test sets.31 Emerging universal semantic frameworks for LLM evaluation, such as the SemBench project, are actively attempting to address these contamination metrics systematically.26  
Secondly, while initial multimodal experiments successfully utilized late-fusion methodologies to align vision-language processing in Basque, the absolute ceiling for multimodal performance remains artificially constrained by the reliance on synthetic data.14 Translating English image-text datasets forces the model to map English visual contexts and cultural artifacts to Basque tokens, entirely neglecting the localized visual realities, geography, and distinct cultural items of the Basque Country. Future research must prioritize the organic collection of native Basque multimodal data to achieve true cultural alignment.  
Finally, the constraints of human alignment pose ongoing challenges. The creation of the *Ebaluatoia* preference dataset marks a monumental first step in accurately modeling Basque human preference.12 However, optimizing LLMs to accurately reflect localized cultural values, political neutrality, and complex ethical norms requires continuous scaling. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) loops must be heavily populated by annotations from diverse native speakers. Relying on translations of English alignment data risks importing implicit Anglo-centric ethical frameworks and conversational norms directly into the Basque models, effectively overriding the cultural sovereignty the project aims to protect.

## **Conclusions**

The exhaustive and systematic evaluation of Large Language Models for the Basque language reveals a rapidly maturing technological ecosystem transitioning aggressively from theoretical exploration to highly viable, open-source deployment. The integration of continual pretraining methodologies on robust, parameter-dense architectures like Meta's LLaMA 3.1 has successfully bridged the performance gap that previously rendered low-resource language processing commercially and academically non-viable.  
Extensive empirical evidence extracted from localized NLU benchmarks like BasqueGLUE and the culturally grounded Latxa Evaluation Suite confirms that bespoke, continually pretrained models decisively and consistently outperform default multilingual configurations. The academic data strongly suggests that the most efficient, compute-optimal pathway for low-resource LLM development is the utilization of an already instruction-tuned English backbone. Subjecting this backbone to continual pretraining on a rigorously filtered, high-quality target-language corpus—such as EusCrawl or ZelaiHandi—effectively maps the complex syntactic rules of the language onto the model's existing logical frameworks. Subsequently, alignment via high-quality synthetic instruction pairs and native preference data (like the Ebaluatoia dataset) solidifies the model's utility as a conversational agent.  
Furthermore, the expansion of Basque NLP into multimodal domains proves that complex cognitive associations between visual and textual inputs successfully transfer across language barriers in the latent space. This dynamic drastically reduces the total volume of native multimodal data required to achieve baseline proficiency, opening new avenues for rapid visual-linguistic development. However, as these open-weight models approach human parity in formalized language understanding tasks, the evaluation frontier must necessarily shift toward greater complexity. The reliance on translation for evaluation risks masking deeper, structural reasoning deficits. To ensure the technological sovereignty, ethical alignment, and digital survival of language isolates like Basque, future research must remain steadfastly focused on generating massive, natively sourced conversational datasets. Embracing informal dialects, code-switching phenomena, physical commonsense reasoning, and localized cultural knowledge will ensure that the artificial intelligence of the future genuinely reflects the linguistic diversity of the populations it serves.
